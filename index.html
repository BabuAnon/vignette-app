Vignette — Complete Guide
What This App Is
Vignette is a clinical reasoning trainer for NEET PG and INICET. It is not a question bank. It does not replace PrepLadder, Marrow, or DAMS. It is the layer on top of those tools that converts passive watching and reading into active, durable memory.
The problem it solves
You can watch a PrepLadder lecture three times and still blank on the question in the exam. This is not laziness or a bad memory. It is the illusion of mastery — recognition feels like knowledge but is not the same thing. Vignette forces retrieval, which is what builds durable memory. The discomfort of trying to recall something you are not sure you know is exactly the mechanism. Do not try to reduce it.
The two tools you need
Use your question bank (PrepLadder, Marrow, DAMS) for video lectures, reading, and full-length grand tests. Use Vignette for daily spaced retrieval and clinical reasoning practice. Both are necessary. Neither replaces the other.
The Algorithm — FSRS
Vignette uses FSRS — Free Spaced Repetition Scheduler — the algorithm that replaced SM-2 as Anki's default in 2023. It is significantly more accurate than the older algorithm.
Every card has three values: Difficulty (how hard this specific card is for you), Stability (how many days until your recall probability drops to your target), and Retrievability (your predicted probability of recalling it right now). After every review your rating updates these values and the algorithm calculates when to show the card next.
Cards you keep getting right are shown less frequently — the intervals grow. Cards you keep missing return daily until you get them right. The schedule adapts to your actual memory, not a fixed timetable.
Revision deadline cap
Every subject has a revision deadline. If the algorithm calculates a next review date after your deadline, it automatically pulls the card forward to fit within the remaining time. No card will be scheduled after your revision deadline.
High Yield boost — topics and subtopics only
Topics and subtopics flagged as High Yield receive a 0.7× interval multiplier — their intervals grow more slowly, keeping them in frequent rotation. NEET PG has well-documented high-yield topics. Flag them when you add them. Subjects themselves are not flagged — only the individual topics and subtopics within them.
Subject Tree — Four Levels of Nesting
Each subject contains topics. Each topic can contain subtopics. Each subtopic can contain further subtopics. Up to four levels deep. Every level can be independently processed and have its own cards.
Example: Medicine → Nephrology → Glomerulonephritis → FSGS → Management of FSGS. You process and create cards at whatever level makes sense for the material. A broad topic like Nephrology might have overview cards. A specific entity like FSGS will have detailed cards at the deepest level.
Recommended setup
Add all your subjects on day one with the correct revision deadlines. Flag Medicine, Surgery, OBG, Pathology, Pharmacology, and Microbiology topics as High Yield generously — these subjects together account for the majority of NEET PG questions. Add topics progressively as you cover them in your primary resource, not all at once.
Revision Deadline
Each subject has a revision deadline — the date by which you want to have finished revising that subject. This is a personal milestone, not the exam date. All subjects are tested on the same exam day, but you may want to finish revising Medicine by Month 3 and Surgery by Month 4. Set deadlines accordingly.
The FSRS cap ensures no card is scheduled after your deadline. If you finish revising a subject ahead of schedule, extend the deadline and the algorithm will spread the remaining reviews appropriately.
Gamification — What the Numbers Mean
Day Streak — consecutive days on which you completed at least one retrieval session. Resets to 0 if you miss a day. The goal is not the streak. The streak is a byproduct of daily retrieval practice.
Weekly Cleared — the percentage of cards that were due this week that you actually reviewed. 100% means you cleared your entire due queue every day. Below 70% means overdue cards are accumulating faster than you are clearing them.
Today's due cards cleared — the progress bar in the Session tab showing how many of today's due cards you have reviewed in the current session. Updates in real time.
Total Cards — running count of all cards in your bank across all subjects.
None of these numbers should become the goal. A student who clears cards dishonestly to protect their streak has been failed by the gamification. The numbers reflect genuine practice — they have no value if the underlying practice is dishonest.
The Two Card Types
Knowledge cards — for drug mechanisms, diagnostic criteria, investigation reference ranges, pathological processes, surgical anatomy, classification systems, and isolated facts. Three fields:
Q
Question — Ask "why" not "what." "Why is the anion gap elevated in lactic acidosis" not "What is lactic acidosis." Questions that test understanding produce stronger memory traces than questions that test recognition.
A
Answer — Concise. Your own words, not the textbook's. If you cannot say it in your own words you have not understood it yet.
C
Context — required, not optional. What existing knowledge does this connect to? This field is what makes the card retrievable six months from now when you have forgotten the context of the original lecture. Never skip it.
Do not copy-paste from notes or textbooks. Cards built from copied text produce recognition, not retrieval. Write the question and answer in your own words after you have understood the concept. If you cannot rephrase it, go back and understand it first.
Vignette cards — for clinical scenarios that mirror NEET PG and INICET question format exactly. Seven fields:
1
Patient Profile — Age, sex, occupation or relevant social history where it matters clinically. "45-year-old male farmer" activates different clinical thinking than "22-year-old female student." Include what is diagnostically relevant.
2
Presenting Complaint — What brought the patient in and for how long. Duration is often the diagnostic key — sudden onset versus gradual onset changes the differential entirely.
3
Key Findings — Three to four findings, one per line. Choose the discriminating features — findings that point toward the correct diagnosis but also activate the most common wrong answer. If your findings only point toward the right answer without activating a distractor, the card is too easy to be useful exam preparation.
4
Question Stem — What you are asking. Most likely diagnosis, investigation of choice, drug of choice, next step in management, mechanism, contraindication, complication. Be specific.
5
Time Target — NEET PG averages approximately 60 seconds per question. INICET averages approximately 45 seconds. The countdown is visible during retrieval. Speed of clinical reasoning is being tested, not just accuracy.
6
Answer — The correct answer. Specific. Not "workup" but "serum amylase and lipase." Not "surgery" but "emergency appendectomy."
7
Reasoning — required. Why this answer and not the most common wrong answer. This is the most important field in the entire app. The exam is designed to catch students who know the right answer but not the reasoning. The distractor logic is always shown after reveal — even when you got the card right. Read it every time.
Where to get vignettes from
Build vignette cards from questions you got wrong in grand tests. From cases you saw during internship. From clinical vignettes in your question bank explanations. The vignettes you build from your own wrong answers are the most valuable because they address your specific failure points, not the generic population's.
The Six Tabs — How To Use Each
Subjects
Your clinical map. Add subjects, set revision deadlines, build your topic tree. Topics and subtopics at any depth up to four levels. Flag high-yield nodes individually. The progress bar shows what percentage of nodes in that subject have been processed.
Session
Open this every time you sit down to study. Three things to do here:
1
Energy check-in first. High energy means new material — process new nodes and create new cards. Medium energy means revision — work through today's scheduled retrieval. Low energy means easy reviews only — cards already rated Got It that are due for a light pass. Do not force deep processing on low energy. You will encode poorly and waste the session.
2
Note the session ratio. The app recommends a Knowledge-to-Vignette ratio based on your nearest revision deadline. Early preparation favours Knowledge cards. Within 60 days the ratio shifts toward Vignette. Within 30 days it shifts heavily toward Vignette. This is a recommendation, not a restriction.
3
Work through the schedule in order. Overdue cards appear first. Due cards second. New cards last. The schedule mixes subjects — this is interleaving, one of the most evidence-backed techniques for durable learning. Do not reorder by subject. NEET PG tests all subjects simultaneously. Your preparation should too.
Process
The gate between studying and creating cards. You cannot create cards for a node without completing the four clinical questions first. This cannot be bypassed. This is the most important feature in the app.
Before the four questions, you are asked what you already know about the node from your MBBS and internship. Write everything — even half-memories. This activates prior knowledge and creates hooks for the new material.
1
Pathophysiology — What is actually happening in the body? Not the name. The mechanism at the cellular or physiological level. If you cannot explain the mechanism you are memorising a fact, not understanding a concept.
2
The Differentiator — What single finding distinguishes this from the most common alternative diagnosis? This forces you to think about the differential simultaneously with the diagnosis. It is what the exam tests with its distractors.
3
Investigation of Choice — One investigation and what it shows. The discipline of one answer trains the clinical habit of reaching for the most diagnostic investigation first.
4
Management Pivot — Standard management and when it changes. NEET PG and INICET are famous for testing the exception: the patient who would normally receive Treatment A but cannot because of Condition B.
Processing takes time. A node processed properly takes 10-15 minutes. A node processed quickly takes 3 minutes and produces cards that fail at retrieval. The processing time is not wasted — it is the encoding.
Cards
Create cards only after processing a node. Use Knowledge cards for mechanisms, criteria, ranges, and isolated facts. Use Vignette cards for clinical entities where the exam will test you through a patient scenario.
A complete node should have both card types. Knowledge cards build the foundational layer. Vignette cards build the clinical reasoning layer on top. A node with only Knowledge cards has gaps in clinical application. A node with only Vignette cards has gaps in foundational knowledge.
How many cards per node?
There is no target number. Create as many cards as there are distinct ideas worth retaining. Quality matters more than quantity. Ten well-built cards outlast fifty poorly-built ones.
Retrieve
The core of the app. Open Retrieve every day.
For Knowledge cards: Read the question. Write everything you recall without looking at anything. Then reveal the answer. Rate yourself honestly using the four-option scale — Blackout, Missed It, Partial, Got It. Any rating below Partial resets the card to day 1.
For Vignette cards: The scenario appears with a countdown timer. Read the patient, findings, and question stem. Write your answer and reasoning before revealing. Three separate checks after revealing — got the answer, got the reasoning, arrived within time. Getting the answer right for the wrong reason is Partial, not Got It.
The distractor logic — always read it
After revealing a Vignette card, the distractor logic is always shown — even when you got the card correct. Read it every time. The exam will present you with variations of the same scenario where the distractor is the correct answer. Knowing why the wrong answer is wrong in this case teaches you when it is right in a different case.
Do not skip the retrieval attempt before revealing the answer. The entire mechanism of the app depends on the effort of trying to recall. Students who reveal the answer first and then read and agree with it are doing passive review, not active retrieval. The discomfort of not being sure is not a problem to be solved. It is the learning happening.
Weekly Review
Do this every Sunday. Two to three hours. Non-negotiable.
The Review tab shows your session statistics, the Confidence vs Performance Gap analysis, the Grand Test reminder, and four questions.
Confidence vs Performance Gap: The app calculates your actual FSRS performance per subject and compares it to your self-reported confidence. A large positive gap — high confidence, poor performance — means the illusion of mastery is operating. This is the most dangerous state in exam preparation.
The Grand Test reminder
Once a week sit a full-length timed mock exam on your question bank platform — 200 questions, timed, exam conditions. The grand test reveals how your knowledge performs under full-exam pressure. After each grand test, create Vignette cards from questions you got wrong or found difficult. This is the most efficient use of grand test feedback.
Common Mistakes to Avoid
Creating cards before processing. The app prevents this technically but the temptation exists to rush through processing to unlock cards quickly. If your processing answers are one line each, you have not processed — you have typed. Proper processing answers are three to five sentences of genuine clinical reasoning.
Skipping the retrieval attempt. Revealing the answer before writing your response and then reading and agreeing is passive review. The mechanism requires the attempt. Without it you are using a spaced repetition tool as a reading tool.
Being dishonest in self-rating. Rating Partial as Got It because you got the core right even though you missed the reasoning costs you. When in doubt, rate down. The algorithm can only schedule what you actually know.
Treating Vignette as a replacement for grand tests. Vignette handles daily retrieval. Grand tests handle full-exam simulation under time pressure. Both are necessary.
A Note on Discomfort
This app is designed to be uncomfortable. Trying to recall something you are not sure you know feels worse than watching a lecture or reading a summary. It should. That discomfort is the desirable difficulty that produces durable memory.
Students who use retrieval practice consistently underestimate how much they have learned because the knowledge is genuinely encoded rather than just recognised. Students who reread consistently overestimate how much they have learned because familiarity feels like knowledge.
If every session feels smooth and comfortable, the app is not working. If sessions frequently produce the experience of trying to recall something and failing — and then seeing the answer and recognising it — the app is working exactly as it should.
